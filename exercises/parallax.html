Forms in Flux: Parallax Perspectives in Contemporary Art

The Tactility of Process

In recent decades, artists have turned increasingly to the process itself as a form of expression. Instead of concealing technique, they foreground it—using thick layers of paint, scorched canvas, broken ceramics, and raw textile remnants to tell stories of imperfection and vulnerability.

This material-first approach shifts focus away from pristine outcomes. The act of making becomes the art, and every fingerprint, burn mark, or tear is embraced as part of the final piece. The result is deeply human, resisting industrial polish and inviting tactile engagement.

By emphasizing decay and transformation, these works reflect the impermanence of physical forms. They become temporal documents—holding space for entropy as a creative force rather than a flaw.

Through the use of unconventional materials and layered methods, contemporary artists are reclaiming messiness as meaning. This shift away from sterile perfection not only critiques mass production—it reaffirms the body’s role in creation and the intimacy of imperfection.
